# Spatial-Temporal Forecasting

Spatial-temporal forecasting is a technique for predicting outcomes by analyzing both spatial and temporal data patterns. It is particularly applied in weather-commodities research, where it helps model complex interactions between environmental factors (e.g., climate signals, satellite data) and market dynamics (e.g., crop yields, commodity prices). This approach integrates geospatial information (such as remote sensing data) with time-series analysis to improve the accuracy of predictions in domains like agriculture and resource management [[sources/mcp-research-team-paper-assignment-profiles.md]].

The technique is critical for research areas involving environmental and economic forecasting, as it enables the modeling of phenomena that evolve across both geographic regions and time. For example, in weather-commodities research, spatial-temporal forecasting can help anticipate how weather patterns affect agricultural production, which in turn influences commodity markets. This method is especially relevant for [[entities/rafael-gallegos]], whose work in crop yield ML and climate signals relies on such approaches [[sources/mcp-research-team-paper-assignment-profiles.md]].

This concept is closely tied to [[entities/rafael-gallegos]]’s research focus and the [[entities/berkeley-weather-pipeline]] infrastructure, which provides the data foundation for spatial-temporal analyses in weather and commodities. It also connects to broader efforts in [[facts/rafael-gallegos-weather-commodities]] research, where integrating spatial and temporal dimensions enhances predictive models for agricultural and market outcomes [[sources/mcp-research-team-paper-assignment-profiles.md]].
